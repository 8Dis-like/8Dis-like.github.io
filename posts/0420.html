If we look back at the history of Europe over the past 1,500 years, it is not difficult to see that economic status ultimately determines everything. In the feudal era (between the fall of the Roman Empire and the French Revolution), Europe did not have a powerful king (even the authority of the Pope was often challenged). This Europe was actually controlled by a group of feudal lords. These dukes, earls, and knights had supreme power in their territories. They had their own castles to control their territories. They enacted laws and levied taxes in their territories. Artisans, merchants, and usurers lived near the castles, which could not only meet the needs of the lords but also have shelter in times of danger - the castle. Cities were not important in the Middle Ages, but in history, they were the center of the country in ancient Egypt, Babylon, Greece, and Rome.
The Crusades changed Europe unconsciously. People migrated from rural areas in western and northern Europe to the Mediterranean coast. People discovered that the world was not limited to their small castles and monasteries, but was vast and open. Along the Mediterranean coast, cities redeveloped, and manufacturers and merchants emerged. They slowly accumulated a small amount of money through handicrafts and commerce, which eventually changed their status in medieval society.
In the Middle Ages, feudal lords were land-owning gentry who also served as knights in battle. The Crusades turned the old order of agrarian society on its head. Answering the Pope’s call, these knights marched thousands of kilometers to fight in the Middle East. They were responsible for covering their own expenses—armor, weapons, travel, and food. Though they owned estates, these castle-dwelling nobles had little access to actual cash. As a result, many counts and barons had to borrow money from merchants.
Lenders like Shylock in Shakespeare’s The Merchant of Venice—who sat behind currency exchange counters (called banco, the origin of the word bank)—were more than willing to lend. A noble might casually request hundreds of gold coins, an amount that could represent a lender’s entire life savings. To secure the loan, the lender would often require the noble to mortgage their estate. In the unfortunate event that the noble died during the crusade against the Ottomans, the lender’s investment would be protected. At first, neither party fully realized how dangerous such transactions were for the borrower. In the end, it was the Shylocks of the world who came to own the estates, while the nobles fell into bankruptcy.
The nobles’ downfall wasn’t immediate. Initially, merchants asked only for seemingly trivial privileges in return, such as the right to hunt on noble lands. Desperate for cash, the lords readily agreed, thinking the exchange inconsequential—after all, what harm was there in letting townspeople hunt in the forests if it meant securing a few hundred gold coins?
And so, they unknowingly signed the death warrants for their own authority. Illiterate due to the prevailing anti-education policies of the time, many nobles couldn’t even write their names. Instead, they simply sealed the contracts prepared by their clerks. With everything seemingly in order, the lords-turned-crusaders marched off, full of zeal, to fight the Muslims in the East.
When they returned two years later, they were destitute. Meanwhile, townspeople were freely hunting on their land. Outraged, a count might order his steward to drive the intruders away. The merchants would comply without resistance—at first. But that very night, a polite baker, acting as their representative, might visit the castle. After congratulating the noble on his safe return, he would politely present the original contract bearing the noble’s seal. Swallowing his rage, the count would be forced to concede the townspeople’s right to hunt.
Six months later, wishing to renovate his crumbling castle, the noble once again turned to the merchants. This time, they demanded the right to form their own guilds in the town—autonomous organizations that operated free from the lord’s interference. Though reluctant, the noble had no choice but to accept the deal. Once the castle was repaired, however, he refused to honor the agreement, relying on his own troops to suppress any opposition. The townspeople could do little but protest his breach of contract.
But when the noble next needed money—say, for his daughter’s dowry—he found no one willing to lend. His reputation as “untrustworthy” had preceded him. Forced to humble himself, he had to offer new concessions before securing another loan. Only after promising additional privileges did the townspeople release the funds they held. In the process, they gained rights they had long dreamed of.
In the centuries following the Crusades, similar scenarios played out across Europe, becoming increasingly common. At first, only minor feudal lords were indebted; eventually, even kings were drowning in debt. To maintain their lavish lifestyles, feudal elites—from kings to squires—spent far beyond their means. By the reign of Louis XV, France’s royal debt had become virtually insurmountable.
To secure funds, feudal rulers continually traded away privileges to urban citizens. Cities grew stronger and attracted labor from the surrounding countryside, increasingly replacing castles as the economic centers of society. These shifts in economic power among social classes ultimately contributed to the collapse of the feudal system.
Of course, the transfer of power from rural strongholds to urban centers was slow and often resisted, occasionally erupting into violence. Citizens and artisans were imprisoned or killed; merchants and bankers frequently lost their investments. But the tide of history favored the cities. Towns grew richer and more influential, while feudal lords became poorer and increasingly irrelevant.
The climax of this transformation came with the French Revolution in 1789 when the king’s creditors—the Third Estate—no longer merely wanted repayment; they demanded political power. King Louis XVI refused, and the result was his execution by guillotine.
Some more moderate landowners may have escaped the king’s fate. But the descendants of those early nobles, now sitting in their decaying castles, looked out at the thriving cities and realized their downfall had been sealed the moment their ancestors naively exchanged feudal privilege for wealth. Urban citizens, now disdainful of the nobility, enjoyed the hard-won rights that had been gained through generations of struggle.

One point is only that the rush of attention and funding dedicated to the concerns and needs of the modern American, and later global, consumer was anything but inevitable. It was the product of a set of proclivities and instincts of those early founders, as well as the social and cultural milieu in which they came of age. They had ambition, no doubt. But much of their focus was on the individual, his or her concerns and needs. It was a near-obsessive focus on those concerns and needs and the sheer brilliance of the contraptions and software products constructed to address them that paved the way for another generation of founders, in the first part of this century, who would create the consumer internet. The era of online advertising, photo-sharing apps, and food delivery empires was near. This next generation of innovators would go even further than the prior, abandoning even the pretext of claims to a broader political project, to the liberating potential of technology. They instead entered into afar more mercenary and straightforward service of the material culture of the time. ——Alexander Carp
