How We Work

Mens et Manus
元智慧
发布日期:   2025-10-20
更新日期:   2025-10-21
文章字数:   1.8k
阅读时长:   11 分

Wandering through the fall career fair at Pauley Pavilion, UCLA, I was stunned by the chaos. Stands with mystery logos and riddle names align both sides. Gadgets and souvenirs attract visitors like in a courtyard market. Interviewees dressed in nice suits smoothed into an interview, giving a nice shot in the endless job-hunting journey. Students are asking sharp questions and comparing and weighing choices. Hirers are welcoming everyone, but picky about every resume. Companies with capital behind them are pushing against deadlines to gear the businesses. Guards at each gate are ruthlessly directing visitors to the right entrance. Everyone seems unsatisfied. But who is to be blamed?

The greatest fear comes from the unknown. To overcome it, we need to derive the origin of a job and channel a rationale to face it.

What is a Job

Modern employment—work that requires selling one’s labor and accepting hierarchical instruction to gain monetary income—as a peculiar and historically recent arrangement. This system was culturally reinforced first by the Protestant work ethic, which rationalized diligence and thrift as virtues, and later by consumerism, which sustains the need for continuous work to fund endless consumption and status seeking. This led to the glorification of workaholism, which often results in burnout and a lack of genuine fulfillment.

Literally, the pervasive anxiety observed in the modern job market, evident in the clamor of career fairs and the pervasive sense of dissatisfaction, stems from a fundamental mismatch between traditional expectations and contemporary economic realities. The unknown nature of career success, coupled with the systemic breakdown of old career paths, necessitates a deeper understanding of the true purpose of productive endeavor. The widespread anxiety among the youth, manifesting as internal competition (内卷), complacency (躺平), or struggling (扑腾), rings the alarm that change is necessary.

For the mindset, focus from merely “finding a job” (找工作) to “finding meaningful things to do” (找事做). This shift in mindset is crucial because the traditional model of career success—the “Good Job” myth—is defunct. This myth, which posits that Personal Effort = Good Study = Good University = Good Job = Success, is increasingly unsupported by macro-level shifts such as economic slowdown, technological disruption, and rising social stratification. In contrast, focusing on finding a meaningful endeavor (“事”) inherently provides the individual with three essential benefits often missing in conventional work:

  1. A sense of control and mastery, leading to the immersive state of “Flow” (心流).
  2. Stable interpersonal relationships.
  3. A genuine sense of life meaning by connecting the individual’s efforts to a greater, external goal.

For the action, what is a sustainable and fulfilling endeavor? Here are 3 factors to consider: Interest, Talent, and Social Demand.

Interest always comes first. After all, it’s the thing to do for most of one’s life. Talent endures one’s performance and secures one’s confidence. It’s not necessary to clarify what one’s interests and talents are specifically. An ambiguous direction is enough for moving. If unsure about the direction, never mind, just observe the daily life. Desires are always common for certain groups. Where there is a desire, there is demand, calling for profitable business and giving birth to native jobs. If interest is the number ahead, talent is the following zeros. And social demand is the plus or minus notation even ahead of the number.

Actually it’s not a trick, it’s a habit to fit into this, once get familiar, enthusiasm is triggered by positive feedback. And that’s why the title serial entrepreneur comes into being.

Build us Together

Nowadays, successfully pursuing meaningful endeavors is rarely a solitary venture; it necessitates finding the right environment and collaborators. This section synthesizes the necessity of building and managing relationships, understanding group dynamics, and finding cultural alignment.

Self-Knowledge and Organizational Fit: Individuals must first identify their own internal compass, or values. The text distinguishes between easily quantifiable “resume virtues” (skills, achievements) and enduring “eulogy virtues” (character, integrity). Understanding one’s values is critical for choosing an organization whose “tribal culture” aligns with one’s own. Organizational cultures can be categorized into five stages, ranging from Stage 1 (“Life sucks”), Stage 2 (“My life sucks”), Stage 3 (“I am great”), Stage 4 (“We are great”), Stage 5 (“We are changing history”), which dictates the team’s capacity for cooperation and achievement.

Strategic Networking: Social connectivity is key to accessing the job market, particularly the often-unseen “hidden employment market”. Effective networking relies less on close “strong ties” and more on leveraging “weak ties” (acquaintances), which span different circles and provide diverse, novel information. Overall, personal networks should be strategically managed across three tiers: the massive external network (up to 5000), the stable social group (around 150, the Dunbar number), and the intimate inner circle (about 5, who mutually shape one’s personality).

Here are some tactics for networking grouped by common features.

  • Gender and Diversity: Businesses that prioritize female employees benefit from a “diversity dividend,” as diverse membership provides varied information that helps avoid the pitfalls of groupthink and female has a gift to spread exclusive information. The sources note that women often manage the equivalent of two full-time jobs (career plus domestic labor) and that the modern labor market is increasingly biased against them, especially at higher levels (“glass ceiling”) and lower levels (“sticky floor”). A company’s care for its female employees is a reliable indicator of its commitment to effective incentive mechanisms and employee needs.
  • The Younger Generation: Managing the Gen-Z requires shifting from “poverty power” (money/fear) to “vibe power” (嗨动力). Since this generation is less concerned with material scarcity, motivation relies on providing a sense of excitement, self-expression, and respect, rather than exhibiting “fatherly arrogance” (“爹味”).
  • Mentorship: Finding a suitable mentor (mentor/曼托) is invaluable for rapid professional growth. A good mentor provides not only technical knowledge but also wisdom and high-level guidance, enabling the protégé to overcome challenges and reach a higher state of being.

Don’t judge the above as vulgar and utilitarian cause this is the truth of real world. A genius could only be discovered in the right place. Oppenheimer would trap in his lab mess if he had never transferred into theory research led by Max Born and Niels Bohr, let alone becoming American Prometheus. Tsien Hsue-shen would struggle with MIT’s Edison experimental style without his dream instructor Theodore Von Karman. In fact, thanks to Karman’s support, Tsien could extend his legal visa issued due to Boxer Rebellion Scholar and even get the security clearance to participate in various military-related top secret projects such as fighters and rockets during WWII, from Caltech JPL to Mojave Desert, from Washington to German, which laid the solid foundation for him to construct the modern defense system independently in China.

Work Life Cohesion

In a volatile economy, the focus shifts to adaptation and strategic career management, requiring individuals to integrate their personal lives and professional paths resiliently.

Strategic Career Management and Pivots: Frequent and aimless job hopping is linked to decreased long-term income, while successful salary increases typically occur when changing jobs after three to four years. Before resigning, an individual should test three self-help strategies:

  1. Regain Control: Take back ownership of the work rhythm, recognizing that doing 80% of a job perfectly is often sufficient for success.
  2. Act as an Outsider: View the job from an anthropological or “spy” perspective, focusing on learning the system’s dynamics and accumulating “intelligence” rather than getting bogged down by daily frustrations.
  3. Develop Transferable Skills: Use the current platform, whether a large or small firm, to acquire “wilderness survival skills” necessary for future independent maneuvering.

If change is necessary, one option is internal maneuvering (腾挪) within a large organization, which provides the benefit of switching roles without the high cost of external job transition. Another model is the “Slash Youth” (斜杠青年), where individuals maintain a primary job and support it with profitable side hustles, treating the side job as a necessary “B plan”. Success as a “Slash Youth” requires mastery of deep work—the ability to focus intensely—and disciplined time management, prioritizing important but non-urgent tasks. When facing a professional promotion or complete career change, success depends on transitioning from a technical specialist to a versatile generalist, and strategically prioritizing an immediate “initial decisive victory” (初战必胜) to build confidence and establish credibility.

Opportunities in the New Economic Ecosystem: Long-term career planning requires analyzing Small Trends (小趋势) within Slow Variables (慢变量) like industrialization, urbanization, technology, and population change. This analysis identifies five major “dividends” (opportunities) that reward specialization, adaptation, and sustained effort:

  1. Supply Chain Builder Dividend (补链者红利): Opportunities in manufacturing and technology by focusing on localized innovation to fill critical industrial weak links, driven by the shift toward a security-plus-efficiency economic model.
  2. Maneuverability Dividend (腾挪红利): Gained by leveraging and connecting opportunities between traditional central cities and rising peripheral/smaller cities (“城市自由”).
  3. Life Curator Dividend (生活家红利): Catering to the rising demand for quality living, aesthetics, and personalized services (post-materialistic values).
  4. Time-Space Travel Dividend (时空穿越红利): Capitalizing on China’s unique experience of rapid development by applying that knowledge and experience to succeed in less-developed foreign markets, such as Africa.
  5. Lifelong Learner Dividend (终身学习者红利): This is the prerequisite for all other dividends, awarded to those who continuously cultivate a broad vision (大局观), prioritize long-term growth (long-termism), and adapt their skills through self-directed, practical learning.

Ultimately, the goal is to shift from the paradigm of “getting big and strong” (做大做强) to securing a unique “ecological niche” (生态位) within a segmented market, thereby transforming into a “new species” adapted to the complex, modern economic environment.

Finally, let me wrap up with some voiceover here.

University features its duality: the uppermost field of theory and free minds versus research and application frontier in modern society. I am not saying today’s university degrade from the former like a religious holy to the later like a resume factory. It’s kind of a mixture of both. Since people in the university are on the ground. They have to strike a balance between the two cliché, pursuing fantasy dream versus get wallet feed.

But instead of the cliché work life balance, I’d like to call my core idea as Work Life Cohesion. Originally, work comes from life, life is fulfilled through work. Every life detail deserves care and professional work. Daily notes accumulate to personal brand and become work. Pondering further, we are all embedding ourselves into the grand evolution of civilization, living to realize the destiny of the universe, synchronizing with rhythm of eons.
