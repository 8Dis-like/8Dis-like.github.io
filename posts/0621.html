Golden June arrives at UCLA the way it always does — suddenly, and all at once, as if the whole campus exhaled after finals and remembered how to breathe. Sunlight pools across Dickson Court in thick amber sheets, catching the brickwork of Royce Hall until its twin towers look less like Lombardy and more like something poured from honey. A squirrel skitters along the low wall by Powell Library, pausing mid-sprint to eye a half-eaten bagel with the entitled confidence of a creature who has never once paid tuition or hibernate. Around the fountain, graduates in black gowns pose for photo after photo, their sashes — UCLA blue, sun-bleached gold, the deep maroon of some other affiliation entirely — catching the breeze like pennants on a ship that’s finally making port. Tourists drift between them: a family from Seoul photographing the Romanesque arches with the solemnity of pilgrims, a retired couple from Ohio asking a student where exactly “the famous library steps” are, a group of local teenagers cutting through campus on scooters, utterly unbothered by the historical weight pressing down from every cornice and column. I find myself standing at the edge of it all, in the same noon light that fell on these same bricks back in 1929, thinking about how strange and lucky it is — that a place built in such a specific, fleeting decade of ambition and concrete and borrowed Mediterranean dreams should still, a hundred years on, feel like exactly the right place to end a year, take a photo, and pause for a while.

As I moved through the venues of the past, so does with my memory. Come to think of it, I’ve spent a full year at UCLA, 4 seasons in total. Thanks to the geographical advantage, I could cover most of the interests in north Los Angeles as well. In spite of the Master title in the end, sometimes I indeed treat my experiences as an undergraduate within. With respect to my last memoir on Wuhan university exactly 1 year ago before graduation, this is the equivalence for UCLA!

Legacy

UCLA’s fame arrived in my mind long before I did. I knew Royce Hall’s open loggias before I knew which bus route got me to Westwood — they’d already flickered across a hundred screens, stood in for a hundred other places, played host to a hundred fictional graduations that weren’t mine. There’s a particular vertigo in walking past a building for the first time and feeling like you’re the one trespassing on its set. Hollywood is twenty minutes away by freeway but it might as well live inside the bricks themselves; this campus has spent a century being borrowed, filmed, captioned, reposted, hash-tagged into a kind of architectural celebrity that exists slightly apart from the people who actually study here. Rookies drill their performance at the Spring Sing stage, release their records as debut trial, get recognized by esteemed judges who also went through this before to become an alumni, paving the path for new stars, inheriting the network for reputation. Every Bruin learns to hold two images of the same place at once: the UCLA you actually live in, with its labyrinth lecture halls and parking permit anxieties, and the UCLA that exists as pure surface online — the Undie Run in terms’ last week, the Janss Steps at golden hour, the fountain selfie that fifty thousand strangers will scroll past without ever learning your name. I spent a year learning to live inside both versions simultaneously, sometimes proud of the borrowed glamour, sometimes faintly embarrassed by it, mostly just amused that a 1929 Romanesque fantasy of Lombardy has become, against all odds, one of the internet’s most recognizable backdrops.

Mentioning the Internet, do you know UCLA is one of the birthplace of it? It’s OK if you don’t. Since most innovations pop up inconspicuously from mundane labs fitting corners or garages, it’s extraordinary to distinguish the next unicorn from the daily rountine. Legends could be told romantically in hindsight but only the blessed could have been there at the right moment. Even if someone got a chance to reap the low hanging fruits, how it will be remembered in stories is still to be determined. And to be frank, there’s no prophet in reality, it’s totally out of the scope of individual’s capability to predict the complexity of it all. Today, life itself doesn’t need too much advice. One can do anything at anywhere without caring about others’ judge. One can pursue what’s desired from the soul. One can also get respected for pure enthusiasm and tolerated for inevitable failure. Therefore, driven by common interests and philosophy behind, UCLA set the stage for people from all over the world to coexist. Some come for the brand name on the resume, others come to make friends during club games. Foreigners come to live an American life, locals come to build or inherit the glory of family.

hodgepodge

Spending long enough wandering and the postcard version falls apart into something messier and far more interesting. Evers since the infancy phase called “El pueblo de Nuestra Señora la Reina de los Ángeles del Río de Porciúncula“, the city unfolded its dynamical evolution. UCLA is of no exception, it’s not one decade frozen in brick — it’s a hundred years of additions stacked on top of each other with only partial regard for harmony. Coincidentally at the same time Wuhan University thrives on the other side of the planet, most Westwood communities laid their foundation during the roaring 1920s. Successfully lobbied to relocate the main campus around the slope, UCLA expands the capacity from its original normal college. Although the Great Depression dealt a heavy blow to the economy, the public school construction continues thanks to the New Deal by President Franklin Roosevelt. Boosting from WWII, the campus grow with the country. Just walk south from Powell Library and the Italian Mediterranean dream gives way, block by block, to the flat concrete pragmatism of the 1950s, then the brutalist confidence of the 60s, then whatever glass-and-steel logic governs the newest buildings going up now. At the end of the day, you dive into the Spanish style business center at the south. It’s less a single architectural statement than an argument that’s been running for a century, each generation answering the last without ever quite agreeing.

Extending along Metro Route 2 eastward outside on the map, we pass by Beverly Hills known for luxury villas sided national cemetery for veterans, Hollywood where theaters and guilds thrive, then Silverlake with every basketball court honoring Kobe Byrant, finally University of South California campus, which fence out the homeless to guard the nobility. Taking the naked Metro C line, we can sneak from the once Staple Center into the underground of Downtown LA. This is exactly the metropolis of the whole city in total sense, where skyscrapers uphold the ground between valleys and ocean. Art museum, concert halls, sculptures, and graffiti jointly define the identity of Los Angeles. Taco trunks ring the bells in front of the masterpieces from architect Frank O. Ghery. Streets are named simply numbers or the sponsors. Union Station rails along the highway and river, connecting adobes and stalls with passengers and goods, separating zones for different races. Strictly speaking, Los Angeles doesn’t own all the lands under one roof. LA county composes of more than 70 sub-cities including LA city, Santa Monica, Pasadena… At a glimpse of the administrative division, it looks typically like a hodgepodge. This is also the reason why LA won’t have a mayor as powerful as New York to unite all departments. Nowhere does that hodgepodge feel more alive than the Los Angeles Central Library or any of the city’s modest branch libraries scattered through neighborhoods that have nothing to do with university prestige. There, the architecture stops being about legacy and starts being about function in the most immediate sense: rows of new immigrants — Korean, Armenian, Salvadoran, Bangladeshi — bent over ESL worksheets, filling out citizenship forms, asking the reference desk how to prepare for the Naturalization test. The public library doesn’t perform history the way UCLA’s quad does. It simply does the unglamorous work of turning strangers into Angelenos, one library card at a time. Somewhere between the borrowed European grandeur and the low-key American civic utility, I started to understand that Los Angeles architecture isn’t trying to look like one thing — it’s trying to hold everyone who’s passing through.

Angelino

Which brings me, eventually, to whatever it means to belong here — even briefly, even as a graduate student who sometimes still feels like an undergrad sneaking into someone else’s golden sunset. Los Angeles doesn’t ask you to assimilate so much as it asks you to coexist: surfers and screenwriters, old money trustees and various brokers, venture capitalists and day laborers, third-generation Angelenos and people who landed at LAX just days ago, all sharing the same stretch of Pacific light. There’s an unguarded, palm-surrounded, sun-warmed honesty to how people live here — beach bodies and bus benches in the same frame, SoFi Stadium and Olympic Village gazing each other, ambition and exhaustion sharing a sidewalk, nobody particularly shocked by anyone else’s reinvention. The city blooms in every direction at once: jacaranda trees dropping purple confessions onto parked cars, food trucks translating a dozen cuisines into one parking lot, a Sunday hike crowded with hijabs and crop tops and dog leashes in equal measure. To be an Angeleno, I’ve come to think, is simply to let all of that stay true at the same time without needing to resolve it. UCLA, for one strange and golden year, gave me a quiet brick corner from which to watch the whole sprawling, blossoming contradiction of it — and somehow, by the end, made me feel like part of it too.

As the juniors begin their summer session just like me 2 years ago. I do resonate with the silhouette of who I used to be by reviewing the documents I spare in my PC. I have standard answers for interview questions like why you chose to come? But I seldom hold faith in it. I just admire the “UCLArchitecture”, which carves out the core of modern society. Settling down my inner voice, I could accept it without meaning, peacefully enjoy being, and embrace what’s coming.
