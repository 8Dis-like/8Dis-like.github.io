Independence Declaration

Mens et Manus 大道行思

Independence, where the United States was born, brews a man who comes here.

Departure

I remember the last departure last year, when I was alone, tightly transferring from Shenzhen North Station to Hong Kong International Airport. Everything was brand new and embracing. I was like a kid freed in a giant garden, opening my eyes to figure out how the world is operating. But this time, it’s quite different. I am departing from Shenzhen Baoan Airport thanks to the low-price ticket from Air China perfectly matching my move-in date. Therefore, my parents decided to drive me to the airport and see me off. Since I am familiar with all the steps along the way from home in China to UCLA in the US, I am much more confident but less excited. Instead, my attention falls on the formal farewell with my parents.

Ever since I have been feverish for the last days before departure, mom and dad each spare particular time to talk about the stories of their generations, encouraging me to compose my own epics, which inevitably adds a lot of weight to this trip. Right at the exact date, they both appear to be irregular. Mom is more silent, while Dad is more optimistic. Although they both share this seeing off right in front of the China Customs, they keep looking at my back moving further and further until the end of their sight. At that moment, I hesitated to look back, only to find their expectation in contrast with my trepidation. In comparison, my girlfriend seemed more excited despite her hidden worries about our years-long separation. Never thought my tears would burst out after the plane took off, when I was suddenly offline to my old contacts, my homeland, and my past life.

Settlement

Diving into the ocean from Shanghai, cutting across Honshu Island, Japan, and edging the North Pacific, I was excited again for witnessing the typical Bay Area, marking my revisit to California. A few hours later, I set foot on the other continent. After a few minutes to pass the US Customs, I reclaimed my giant suitcases and struggled to take an Uber. Aiming towards my apartment, LA took off the golden cloak at dusk and put on the veil of clear night. But for me, there’s no time for rest, actually, the rush hours have just come to challenge.

Thanks to my familiarity from my last visit and proper arrangements based on that experience, I got my permanent US phone number for the first time and opened a BOA (Bank of America) account for the second time. Nonetheless, to totally settle on a new land on the other side of the Pacific Ocean is no easy feat. Everything runs by different rules, and I have to embed myself into them. Learning from the previous experience of helping my girlfriend move in, on the first night, I just tidied up the bed to secure a good sleep to handle the time lag. After that, I dashed into 99 Ranch to gather up cooks ASAP. Then on the third day, fresh foods come from Trader Joe’s along with the fast Weee delivery, I start to feed myself with my newly acquired cooking skills during the summer.

Looking backwards, the rush hours culminate this adventure. I relied on mooncakes, hamburgers, and even instant noodles to extend the game. I triaged various transports from Waymo to Bird to carry my supplies from supermarkets. I rewarded myself with delicious Teriyaki Rice by Doordash and drinking water by Uber Eats. I collaborated with my girlfriend to pick up my life and shared my sightseeing. Despite some sad moments, I am not alone to bear through. When spending exceeded the budget, my dad encouraged me to look forward. When disappointed, mom and girlfriend comfort with their relevant stories. When stuck in a niche predicament, I am lucky to receive help from roommates, cousins, American friends, and netizens from the Rednote. I am here to express my sincerest gratitude to all the helping hands mentioned above.

Go Bruins

Bruins, the mascot of UCLA, AKA the symbol of being a family member of UCLA, stands right at the Bruin Plaza. Featuring the accumulated reputation and the absorbing culture in Los Angeles, UCLA embraces thousands of students from all over the world every year. During welcome orientation, students of different skin colors share their backgrounds. Organized by the Chinese student union, strangers gather together to enjoy the sunlight at Venice Beach. Triggered by tiny ideas, passengers join the street dancing. Fans culting for a visiting Hollywood star, UCPD has to cut in to maintain order. Apart from that, UCLA provides access to extensive services, including health insurance, recreation arenas, writing tutorials, career fairs, and even a public transit pass. The stage is here, inviting souls with courage and minds to come.

Once settled, it’s time to dream big. I completed the tuition payment and an international transfer for next season’s rental at the most favorable exchange rate right after the Federal Reserve rate cuts. I throw myself into IT circles by paying for exclusive resources while engaging directly in job applications and online assessments. I evaluated candidate courses for the coming quarter and prepared full materials ahead of time. I achieved more and more, declaring my independence enthusiastically, only to find that while I got further, I am on my own.

Life in the US is much slower than in China. People here value privacy and work-life balance. Since I have decided to come here, I have to get used to it. To some extent, all the people coming here are immigrants pursuing their dreams respectively. They never get ashamed of making money, even talking about it in a public speech on campus. Each person charts a unique course while interacting temporarily at the intersection. Once they have something in common, they unite and join forces to create a union, an organization, a city, or even a country. Different from the military parade, which is top-down, neat and orderly, people here cherish the bottom-up, chaotic and creative, which leads the technological innovation and the international governance system for today’s world.

At this point, I don’t embellish too much to draw a bigger picture, nor do I live too profiteering to spin like a top. Since life is for the soul to live out. Each step could teleport to an unpredictable future. Once your step resonates with the keynote of the age, magic could be cast. Go Bruins!

Finally, stick here with the extraordinary introduction of Thread of the Silkworm by Iris Chang.

The story of Tsien Hsue-shen is epic in scale, encompassing some of the greatest technological and political convulsions of this century. It winds from the crumbling of a four-century-old dynasty in China to the terror of Japanese air raids over Shanghai, from the secret American missile tests in a dry river bed in southern California to the deadly concentration camp factories of the V-2rocket in Germany, from Tsien’s imprisonment on a small island in the United States tohis conferences with the most powerful members of the Soviet Union and China. It is the story of one of the most monumental blunders the United States committed during its shameful era of McCarthyism, in which the government’s zeal for Communist witch-hunting destroyed the careers of some of the best scientists in the country. It is the story of Tsien’s scientific achievement and leadership, and how it helped propel not only the United States but his homeland of China into the space age. It is a story of how Tsien was born into a nation of rickshaws and left it a nation of rockets within the span of a single lifetime. It is also the dramatic story of the Chinese struggle to build a nuclear missile and space program amidst decades of internal political chaos. And finally, it is also the private story of a shy, introspective, brilliant scientist who wanted nothing more in life than to work in peace but was caught up not once, but twice, in the vortex of world politics.
